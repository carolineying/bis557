Consider the simple regression model with only a scalar x and intercept: \[ y = \beta_0+\beta_1\cdot x \] Using the explicit formula for the inverse of a 2-by-2 matrix, write down the least square estimators for \(\hat{\beta_0}, \hat{\beta_1}\).

The MSE\((\beta_0,\beta_1) = \frac{1}{n} \sum\limits_{i=1}^n (y_i - (\beta_0+\beta_1x_i))^2\)

We have \(\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}\beta + \epsilon\), where \[ \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ ... \\ y_n \end{pmatrix} \text{ , } \mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_1 \\ 1 & x_2 \\ ... & ... \\ 1 & x_n \end{pmatrix} \text{ , } \mathbf{\beta} = \begin{pmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \end{pmatrix} \text{ and } \epsilon = \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_1\\ \epsilon_2\\ ...\\ \epsilon_n \end{pmatrix} \] Solving the least square equation directly, we get \(\hat{\beta} = (X^TX)^{-1}Xy\) \[ X^TX = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & ...&1 \\ x_1 & x_2 & ... &x_n \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_1 \\ 1 & x_2 \\ ... & ... \\ 1 & x_n \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} n & \sum\limits_{i=1}^n x_i \\ \sum\limits_{i=1}^n x_i & \sum\limits_{i=1}^n x_i^2 \end{pmatrix} \]
